Limitations Are Self-Imposed

july 30, 2025

I hate the phrase “I can’t.” Let me tell you why.

This aversion comes from a lifelong frustration with predetermined boundaries—the closed-system scripts that dictate what you're supposed to do and who you're supposed to be. Growing up, any deviation felt like ruining a communal play, an outdated script everyone kept performing out of habit. I wanted to step off the stage and draw my own path.

It’s this same frustration I felt when a friend dismissed the idea of pivoting to product management by saying, “I can’t, I don’t code.” My immediate reaction was confusion: “Then learn?” The word “can’t” often isn’t an accurate assessment of possibility; it’s a self-imposed limit placed before the attempt.

For a long time, I couldn’t explain why I felt so passionate about New York City. The answer revealed itself during my second time living there: everyone drawn to NYC shares this same aversion to limitation. Symbolically, the high variation across its neighborhoods and the towering skyscrapers represent a lack of upper boundary. The beauty of New York is that it celebrates multiplicity—you can start a new job in investment banking, pursue acting on the side, and DJ at a local venue on the weekends.

Limits themselves are completely optional—the only purpose they serve is to design your system in a way that produces a more filtered result. My message isn't to condemn those who choose to have them. I believe that judicious filtering is rewarding. However, there is a critical distinction between being filtered and being constrained, and the difference depends entirely on your intention (in operations research, also known as the objective function).

Think about why you are introducing limitations: Do you want to remove the noise, or do you want to minimize uncertainty?

The distinction is that the desire to “remove the noise” stems from confidence in a particular goal, it is a productive approach, while wanting to “minimize uncertainty” is a result of fear of failure or ambiguity. One sharpens focus, while the other shrinks potential.

Limitations were initially created to install structure, but unstructure is where innovation flourishes. Structure is protection—it keeps systems stable and predictable. Yet, there’s a tipping point where protection transforms into complacency and, ultimately, self-constraint. Our bodies provide the perfect analogy: nerves warn us not to overextend by creating pain, much like a flinch when you touch something hot. But touching the heat isn't always bad; it depends on how you perceive it. Fire can burn, yes—but it can also forge, illuminate, or transform. The difference lies in your relationship to the discomfort.

I actually came to this realization doing planks on a Tuesday evening. I’d always believed my limit was a minute and a half. This time, I was so distracted by the music and my own thoughts that I stayed in the position without straining. When I finally looked at the timer, it was two minutes. I didn't feel constrained until I saw the time—the limit wasn't physical; it was purely cognitive.

“I can’t” isn’t a fact; it’s a decision.

Ultimately, your relationship with limits boils down to your objective function: are you using a boundary to remove the noise, or are you seeking to minimize your personal discomfort with uncertainty? One sharpens focus, while the other shrinks potential.
